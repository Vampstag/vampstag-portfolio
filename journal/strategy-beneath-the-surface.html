Strategy Beneath The Surface

M. Dimas K. G.
May 20, 2025 • 4 Min Read
Illustration by Alex Seciu

Most people think brand architecture lives in diagrams.

Boxes connected by thin lines. Clean hierarchy trees carefully arranged inside Figma files. Everything symmetrical. Everything organized. As if clarity alone could solve a business problem.

For a long time, I thought the same way.

I used to believe visual systems were mostly about making things look structured — cleaner logos, better layouts, more consistent feeds. But the more projects I touched, the more I realized something uncomfortable:

Most brand decisions aren’t visible on the surface.

The real strategy happens underneath.

It lives in the invisible tension between products. In the way attention gets distributed across campaigns. In deciding which product becomes the face of the company, which one quietly funds the business, and which one exists purely to protect market position.

A diagram only shows the final arrangement. It rarely explains why the structure exists in the first place.

I stumbled deeper into this idea while reading Pedro Porto Alegre’s Strategy Beneath The Surface, which expands on David A. Aaker’s brand architecture thinking. What interested me wasn’t the terminology. It was the shift in perspective.

It made me realize that visual systems are less like decoration and more like infrastructure.

  • Some brands are designed to generate stability.
  • Some are designed to shift perception.
  • Some are designed to experiment without risking the reputation of the core business.

And suddenly, a portfolio stops looking like a collection of products. It starts looking like a living system with internal politics.

That perspective changed the way I look at creative work.

Take collaborations, for example. Most people only see the visual outcome: the campaign video, the teaser, the packaging, the hype on social media. But underneath all of that is a strategic decision about energy.

Why does a brand collaborate with something culturally loud like Gundam? Why invest heavily into atmosphere, storytelling, and cinematic rollout for a limited release?

Because some campaigns aren’t designed to maximize short-term sales.

They exist to reshape perception.

To inject new emotional energy into the brand. To reposition how people talk about it. To make the audience feel like the brand suddenly belongs in a larger cultural conversation.

That’s what made me rethink visual strategy entirely.

Good visuals are not just decoration for strategy. They are part of the mechanism itself.

But the opposite is also true.

Without strategic thinking, visuals become expensive surface treatment. Beautiful, polished, and ultimately forgettable.

Understanding systems over making content

I think that’s why I’ve become less interested in “making content” and more interested in understanding systems.

Not just: “What should this post look like?”

But: “What role does this piece of content play inside the larger ecosystem?”

  • Does it build trust?
  • Does it shift perception?
  • Does it create relevance?
  • Does it attract attention while protecting the core identity of the brand?

The older I get, the more I realize creative work isn’t really about aesthetics alone.

It’s about designing relationships between perception, emotion, attention, and behavior.

And sometimes, the most important part of a visual system is the part nobody sees.
